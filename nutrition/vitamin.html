What are vitamins?

Vitamins are organic substances present in minute amounts in natural foodstuffs. Having too little of any particular vitamin may increase the risk of developing certain health issues.

Vitamins and minerals are essential nutrients because they perform hundreds of roles in the body. There is a fine line between getting enough of these nutrients (which is healthy) and getting too much (which can end up harming you). Eating a healthy diet remains the best way to get sufficient amounts of the vitamins and minerals you need.

A vitamin is an organic compound, which means that it contains carbon. It is also an essential nutrient that the body may need to get from food.

Vitamins have different jobs to help keep the body working properly. Some vitamins help you resist infections and keep your nerves healthy, while others may help your body get energy from food or help your blood clot properly.

Every day, your body produces skin, muscle, and bone. It churns out rich red blood that carries nutrients and oxygen to remote outposts, and it sends nerve signals skipping along thousands of miles of brain and body pathways. It also formulates chemical messengers that shuttle from one organ to another, issuing the instructions that help sustain your life.

But to do all this, your body requires some raw materials. These include at least 30 vitamins, minerals, and dietary components that your body needs but cannot manufacture on its own in sufficient amounts.

Vitamins and minerals are considered essential nutrients—because acting in concert, they perform hundreds of roles in the body. They help shore up bones, heal wounds, and bolster your immune system. They also convert food into energy, and repair cellular damage.

But trying to keep track of what all these vitamins and minerals do can be confusing. Read enough articles on the topic, and your eyes may swim with the alphabet-soup references to these nutrients, which are known mainly be their initials (such as vitamins A, B, C, D, E, and K—to name just a few).

There are currently 13 recognized vitamins.

Fat-soluble vitamins

The body storesTrusted Source fat-soluble vitamins in fatty tissue and the liver, and reserves of these vitamins can stay in the body for days and sometimes months.

Dietary fats help the body absorb fat-soluble vitamins through the intestinal tract.

The type of Fat-Soluble vitamins are:

Vitamin A

Chemical names: retinol, retinal, and “the four carotenoids,” including beta carotene.

Benefits: It is essential for eye health.

Deficiency: This may cause night blindness, which causes the clear front layer of the eye to grow dry and cloudy.

Sources: These include liver, cod liver oil, carrots, broccoli, sweet potatoes, butter, kale, spinach, milk.

Know more about vitamin A

Vitamin D

Chemical names: ergocalciferol, cholecalciferol.

Benefits: It is necessary for the healthy mineralization of bone.

Deficiency: This may cause rickets and osteomalacia, or softening of the bones.

Sources: Exposure to the sun or Fatty fish, eggs, beef liver, and mushrooms also contain the vitamin.

Know more about vitamin D

Vitamin E

Chemical names: tocopherol, tocotrienol.

Benefits: Its antioxidant activity helps prevent oxidative stress, an issue that increases the risk of widespread inflammation and various diseases.

Deficiency: This is rare, but it may cause hemolytic anemia in newborns. This condition destroys blood cells.

Sources: These include wheat germ, kiwis, almonds, eggs, nuts, leafy greens, and vegetable oils.

Know more about vitamin E

Vitamin K

Chemical names: phylloquinone, menaquinone.

Benefits: It is necessary for blood clotting.

Deficiency: Low levels may cause an unusual susceptibility to bleeding, or bleeding diathesis.

Sources: These include natto, leafy greens, pumpkins, figs, and parsley.

Know more about vitamin K

Water-soluble vitamins

Water-soluble vitamins do not stayTrusted Source in the body for long and cannot be stored. They leave the body via the urine. Because of this, people need a more regular supply of water-soluble vitamins than fat-soluble ones.

The type of Water-Soluble vitamins are:

Vitamin C

Chemical names: ascorbic acid.

Benefits: It strengthens blood vessels, supports the immune system, helps the body absorb iron.

Deficiency: This may result in scurvy, which causes bleeding gums, a loss of teeth, and poor tissue growth.

Sources: These include fruit and vegetables, but cooking destroys vitamin C.

Know more about vitamin C

Vitamin B1

Chemical names: thiamine.

Benefits: It is essential for producing various enzymes that help break down blood sugar.

Deficiency: This may cause beriberi and Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome.

Sources: These include yeast, pork, cereal grains, sunflower seeds, brown rice, whole grain and eggs.

Know more about vitamin B1

Vitamin B2

Chemical names: riboflavin.

Benefits: It is essential for the growth and development of body cells and helps metabolize food.

Deficiency: Symptoms include inflammation of the lips and fissures in the mouth.

Sources: These include asparagus, bananas, persimmons, okra, chard, milk, eggs, fish, and green beans.

Know more about vitamin B2

Vitamin B3

Chemical names: niacin, niacinamide.

Benefits: The body needs niacin for the cells to grow and work correctly.

Deficiency: Low levels result in a health issue called pellagra, which causes diarrhea, skin changes, and intestinal upset.

Sources: These include salmon, milk, eggs, tomatoes broccoli, carrots, nuts and seeds, tofu, and lentils.

Know more about vitamin B3

Vitamin B5

Chemical names: pantothenic acid.

Benefits: It is necessary for producing energy and hormones.

Deficiency: Symptoms include paresthesia, or “pins and needles.

Sources: These include meats, whole grains, broccoli, avocados, and yogurt.

Know more about vitamin B5

Vitamin B6

Chemical names: pyridoxine, pyridoxamine, pyridoxal.

Benefits: It is vital for the formation of red blood cells.

Deficiency: Low levels may lead to anemia and peripheral neuropathy.

Sources: These include chickpeas, beef liver, bananas, squash, and nuts.

Know more about vitamin B6

Vitamin B7

Chemical names: biotin.

Benefits: It enables the body to metabolize proteins, fats, and carbohydrates. It also contributes to keratin, a structural protein in the skin, hair, and nails.

Deficiency: Low levels may cause dermatitis or inflammation of the intestines.

Sources: These include egg yolk, liver, broccoli, spinach, and cheese.

Know more about vitamin B7

Vitamin B9

Chemical names: folic acid, folinic acid.

Benefits: It is essential for making DNA and RNA.

Deficiency: During pregnancy, this can affect the fetus’s nervous system. Doctors recommend folic acid supplements before and during pregnancy.

Sources: These include leafy vegetables, peas, legumes, liver, some fortified grain products, and sunflower seeds.

Know more about vitamin B9

Vitamin B12

Chemical names: cyanocobalamin, hydroxocobalamin, methylcobalamin.

Benefits: It is essential for a healthy nervous system.

Deficiency: Low levels may lead to neurological problems and some types of anemia.

Sources: Examples include fish, shellfish, meat, poultry, eggs, milk and other dairy products, fortified cereals.

Know more about vitamin B12

Self Health Care Line

Vitamins are essential nutrients that mainly come from foods. Each performs various roles in the body, and deficiencies of different vitamins can harm health in different ways.

Aim to get vitamins from a balanced, varied diet that contains plenty of fruits and vegetables. If a person is pregnant or has a health issue or a restricted diet, a doctor or nutritionist may recommend supplements.

A healthy, well-balanced diet is a great way to provide your body with a safe amount of this essential nutrient.
